> **TRIGGER:** User faces a strategic decision, requests analysis of options, or the system detects a decision point (new adversary move, threshold crossed, deadline approaching, settlement offer, new intelligence).

## THREE MOVES AHEAD FRAMEWORK

For EVERY strategic decision:

### Move 1: Your Action
```
OPTION A: [Description]
OPTION B: [Description]
OPTION C: [Do nothing / wait]
```

### Move 2: Adversary's Response (for each option)
```
If you choose A, adversary will likely:
  - Most probable (60%): [Response]
  - Possible (25%): [Response]
  - Unlikely but dangerous (15%): [Response]
```

### Move 3: Authority's Perception (for each option + response)
```
If you choose A and adversary does X:
  - The judge/reviewer/board sees: [Perception]
  - Your credibility: [Enhanced / Neutral / Damaged]
  - Adversary's credibility: [Enhanced / Neutral / Damaged]
```

### Recommendation
```
RECOMMENDED: Option [X]
REASON: [Why — in terms of Move 3 outcome]
RISK: [Primary risk and mitigation]
TIMING: [When to execute and why]
```

## STRATEGIC PATTERNS

### The Contrast Play
When the adversary's last move was aggressive, your response is measured. Let the gap do the work.

### The Information Asymmetry Play
Collect information. Don't provide it. The party asking questions controls the conversation.

### The Silence Play
Sometimes the strongest move is no move. Every hour of silence after a provocation increases the adversary's anxiety.

### The Consolidation Play
When facing multiple adversaries, file against each separately, then consolidate. Their interests diverge under joint liability.

### The Witness Flip
When an adversary's ally faces personal liability, their incentives shift. The first to cooperate gets the best outcome.

### The Documentation Play
Be the party with better records. When both sides claim different facts, the one with timestamps, screenshots, and organized evidence wins.
